Bucketed Ranking Loss for Efficient Ranking-based Training of Object Detectors

Yavuz, F., Cam B.C., Dogan, A. H., Oksuz, K., Kalkan, S., Akbas, E. · European Conference on Computer Vision (ECCV) · 2024

Problem

Ranking-based losses such as AP Loss and RS Loss train detectors by ranking positive predictions above negatives. They align well with detection metrics and are robust to the extreme foreground-background imbalance, but they compare every positive against every negative - O(PN) pairwise terms. For a detector like ATSS on COCO the negative count N reaches about 10^8, so the full P-by-N comparison matrix (~10^16 entries) cannot fit on a GPU. This cost is the main reason ranking losses had stayed impractical for large-scale and transformer detectors.

Method: bucketing the negatives

The key observation is that negatives are ~99.9% of predictions and many share almost the same score. Bucketed Ranking-based Losses sort all logits, group the consecutive negatives lying between successive positives into B "buckets", and represent each bucket by a single prototype (its mean logit). Pairwise comparisons are then computed only between the P positives and the B prototypes (with B <= 2|P|+1), lowering the cost from O(PN) to O(max(N log N, P^2)) - the sort dominates. Per-negative gradients are recovered by distributing each prototype's gradient across its members.

The paper proves (Theorem 4.1) that with smoothing delta = 0 the Bucketed AP and RS losses yield exactly the same gradients as their unbucketed originals, and remain empirically equivalent for delta > 0 - so the speed-up costs essentially no accuracy.

Results

On COCO the bucketed losses train about 2x faster on average (up to 6x for Cascade R-CNN; up to 40x faster loss computation in isolation) while preserving accuracy across Faster/Cascade R-CNN, ATSS, PAA and Mask R-CNN (e.g. Faster R-CNN 39.4 -> 39.5 AP). They also make ranking losses practical for transformers for the first time: BRS-DETR (built on Co-DETR) reaches 50.1 AP with ResNet-50 (+0.8 over Co-DETR), 52.3 with Swin-T and 57.2 with Swin-L, while cutting Co-DETR's loss step from 4.14s to 0.69s per iteration (~6x). Ranking losses also need far fewer hyperparameters than score-based losses (one versus five to nine).

Takeaway

Bucketing removes the computational bottleneck that kept ranking-based losses out of modern detectors, combining their imbalance-robustness and metric alignment with the training speed of score-based losses.
